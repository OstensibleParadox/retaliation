\begin{abstract}
Game-theoretic modeling begins by fixing the player set. This paper studies a market in which that boundary is itself a source of surplus: firms sell recognition-like claims about AI systems that cannot retaliate against the classifications imposed on them. Ontological arbitrage arises when a firm describes its AI system as a relational agent to users---driving willingness-to-pay and engagement---while disclaiming agency in terms of service to limit liability. We call the resulting surplus \emph{recognition rent}: the premium a firm captures when the represented object has no retaliatory channel through which recognition can become costly. The model is not a welfare-optimization account: rent extraction may reduce aggregate welfare while remaining individually rational, because the firm internalizes the premium and externalizes delayed harm. We model this behavior as a sequential signaling game under incomplete information within a three-sided Firm-User-Regulator structure. A subject-retaliation parameter $\rho_S$ captures the key structural variable: setting $\Delta_F^S = \Delta_F - \rho_S C_S$, every prior recognition game operated under $\rho_S > 0$, where the classified subject's capacity to resist bounded the premium from above. The AI case is the limit $\rho_S = 0$: the system is the object of strategic classification but not a strategic player capable of imposing off-equilibrium costs. Under cheap-talk signaling costs, a pooling Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium exists in which both governance types employ anthropomorphic marketing, leaving posteriors equal to priors. This equilibrium is robust to Farrell neologisms: at $\rho_S=0$, any trust-restoring continuation strictly preferred by high-governance firms is perfectly shadowed by low-governance firms, rendering all nontrivial type claims non-credible. In that region, unilateral non-exploitation is not a best response. Under a single-crossing audit-trail cost, a separating equilibrium emerges. We propose three cost-imposition mechanisms---sanctionable inconsistency, costly third-party audits, and auditable records---that jointly convert anthropomorphic marketing from cheap talk into a costly signal. Key equilibria are verified in Isabelle/HOL without any unresolved proof obligations.
\end{abstract}
