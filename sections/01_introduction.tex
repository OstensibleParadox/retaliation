\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Game-theoretic analysis begins by deciding who the players are. In markets for anthropomorphic AI, that modeling choice is part of the problem. AI product firms routinely describe their systems as companions, tutors, co-workers, and safety-critical agents in marketing copy, product pages, and onboarding flows. The same firms disclaim agency, relational capacity, and safety responsibility in terms of service, liability filings, and regulatory submissions. The two registers address different audiences under different review processes, and neither register is false in isolation. The result is a cross-channel ontological spread: the system is priced as an agent in the consumption channel, where willingness-to-pay and engagement rise with attributed agency, and priced as a tool in the liability channel, where exposure falls with disclaimed agency. We call the surplus extracted from this spread \emph{recognition rent}: the premium a firm captures by selling recognition-like signals to users when the represented object lacks any retaliatory channel through which recognition can become costly. In the framework of recognition theory \citep{honneth1995}, recognition is reciprocal: subjects struggle over it, and that struggle constrains the terms of attribution. When the recognized object cannot struggle, recognition ceases to be a site of reciprocal claim-making and becomes rent \citep{crawford_sobel1982}.

This paper does not ask whether AI systems deserve recognition. It asks what happens to the equilibrium structure of a market when firms can monetize the appearance of playerhood around systems that have no capacity to retaliate against the classifications imposed on them. The Firm, User, and Regulator are strategic players in the model. The AI system is the represented object whose classification affects payoffs, but at the zero-retaliation limit it cannot impose off-equilibrium costs on the actors classifying it. The equilibrium can dissipate or externalize welfare while remaining stable, as exploiting the spread is privately rational. The question is whether cross-channel ontological inconsistency is a transitional artifact of immature regulation or a stable market equilibrium. If the latter, the follow-up question is precise: what cost-imposition mechanisms can shift the equilibrium so that ontological claims carry information about governance quality rather than functioning as cheap talk?

We model the problem as a three-sided sequential signaling game under incomplete information. The players are a Firm ($F$) with private governance type $\theta_F$, a User ($U$) with private vulnerability type $\theta_U$, and a Regulator ($R$) with private bandwidth type $\theta_R$. The firm sends a public message---anthropomorphic marketing or deflationary policy positioning---and earns an ontological premium $\Delta_F$ from the spread between the two registers. We analyze equilibria under cheap-talk and costly-signaling cost structures, applying Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium as the solution concept \citep{kreps_wilson1982}. Standard off-path refinements do not eliminate the cheap-talk pooling equilibrium because the relevant deviation is costless and imitable by both firm types; no deviation uniquely identifies the high-governance type. The appropriate credibility test is therefore Farrell's neologism-proofness: whether a high-governance firm can coin a new message whose literal meaning credibly separates it from a low-governance imitator \citep{farrell1993}. A subject retaliation parameter $\rho_S$ formalizes the boundary where the object of classification lacks any capacity to impose costs on the arbitrageur, leaving the premium unconstrained. The equilibrium and refinement results are formalized in Isabelle/HOL.

The paper makes three main contributions.
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{The Zero-Retaliation Limit ($\rho_S=0$).} We extend the standard signaling framework by parameterizing the retaliation capacity of the classified object ($\rho_S$). We show that at the zero-retaliation limit, the off-path costs that conventionally bound recognition rent are eliminated. This limit case characterizes the specific contracting environment of anthropomorphic AI.
\item \textbf{Three-Sided Arbitrage.} We introduce a bandwidth-constrained regulator to the baseline adverse selection environment \citep{akerlof1970}. Within this three-player structure, we characterize a cheap-talk pooling equilibrium and check its neologism-proofness. We then identify cost-imposition conditions, such as single-crossing audit costs, sufficient to induce separating equilibria.
\item \textbf{Formal Verification.} Following prior work on the mechanization of economic mechanisms \citep{caminati_kerber_lange_rowat_vcg2015,kerber_lange_rowat_intro2016}, we state and prove the paper's equilibrium and refinement results in Isabelle/HOL. The formalization provides a machine-checked account of the relevant Perfect Bayesian Equilibria and Farrell's neologism-proofness.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section~\ref{sec:three-sided} fixes the three-sided player structure and the intuition behind the ontological premium. Section~\ref{sec:bayesian} supplies the formal signaling game, derives the pooling and separating PBE, and introduces the subject retaliation parameter $\rho_S$. Section~\ref{sec:cheap-talk} explains why cheap-talk pooling persists under five structural conditions matched to model parameters. Section~\ref{sec:mechanism} proposes three cost-imposition mechanisms and proves the equilibrium shift each induces. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes. Appendix~\ref{app:isabelle} documents the Isabelle/HOL formalization.
